Substance abuse impact on youth mental health

--name April 2, 2014


Drug abuse can have serious health consequences at any age, but teens who use drugs are especially vulnerable. Teens who use drugs are more likely to develop an addiction later in life and to suffer from permanent and irreversible brain damage. Other common negative consequences of teen drug abuse involve:


*Unhealthy coping mechanisms - like Anxiety, depression, mood swings, suicidal thoughts, and schizophrenia can all be caused or masked by drug abuse. In fact, 34.6 percent of teens with major depression report using drugs. Unfortunately, drug abuse can intensify the severity of these emotional issues. Teens who use illicit drugs on a weekly basis, for example, double their risk of depression and anxiety.


*Learning disabilities - Drug abuse harms both short-term and long-term memory and can lead to learning and memory problems later in life.


*Traumatic brain - Teens who use needles to abuse drugs are more likely to contract blood-borne diseases such as HIV, AIDS, and Hepatitis B and C.


*Damage to the brain*Damage to the brain - Teen drug abuse can result in serious mental disorders or permanent, irreversible brain or nervous system damage. Brain damage in drug-addicted teens includes shrinkage of the brain, impaired learning abilities, amnesia and memory problems, impaired reasoning, perception, and intuition, increased or decreased peer interaction, and changes in sexual desire.


*Car accidents - Youngsters who use drugs are more likely to be involved in car accidents that result in injuries or death.


Control and Management


1) Discover effective ways to manage peer pressure


Humans have a tendency to not want to be left out, especially by their friends and peers. As a result, most teenagers and some adults initiate taking drugs in order to fit in with their peers.


If you find yourself in this situation, try to muster up the courage to say no. Better yet, you can make new friends who will not pressure you to adopt their habits. When you are tempted to try drugs, make an excuse that will get you out of the situation.


2) Look for better coping mechanisms


Many people who abuse drugs see it as a coping mechanism for the negative things in their lives. It is critical to understand that there is nothing wrong with feeling negative emotions. Find better ways to deal with them instead of turning to drugs for solace.


Among many of the effective coping mechanisms are


*conversing with someone
*Meditation
*Yoga
*Taking notes on it
*Exercising
*Trying out any of these mechanisms diverts your attention away from drugs.


3) Choose your circle of friends carefully


Make sure you surround yourself with people who love and support you and discourage you from attempting substance abuse. Such people also provide a safe environment in which drug and alcohol risk factors are less likely to be triggered.


4) Always allow yourself some alone time


Imagine spending your whole day by studying and doing all the activities you do and forgetting to give yourself your alone time and resting and doing things you actually love.


During your alone time you can:


*Read your favorite books
*Taking a hot bath
*Distancing yourself from your phones and social media

5) Analyze any possible risk factors


Knowing ahead of time about any potential drug abuse risk factors makes it easier to overcome them. For example, if your family has a history of substance abusers, you can promise yourself that you will not follow in their footsteps. Also, if you have a friend who is a chronic drug user, you can choose to end the friendship rather than being dragged into his behaviors.
